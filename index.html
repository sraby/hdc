Bio


      I'm Sam Raby, a Brooklyn-based mapmaker and interactive media developer with four years experience designing web applications for social change. With a background in math and data analysis, I'm a strong believer that justice, advocacy, and storytelling should inform technology, and not the other way around. I work with GIS mapping, information design, oral history, and data journalism to tell complex stories to large audiences, and I am dedicated to bringing a critical perspective into the world of data and technology.


Past Work: Esri Story Maps, Blue Raster, Curbed (Vox Media)
Current: 596 Acres, Anti-Eviction Mapping Project, Urban Displacement Project

Placing Oral Histories

A Visualization of Four Syrian Refugees' Narratives of Displacement

Under contract with the Esri Story Maps team, I conducted seven in-person interviews with Syrian refugees living across Sweden, Holland, and Germany, and I compiled their stories into long-form digital archives incorporating spatial visualizations, text, and photography.

  • Date: June - October 2017
  • Collaborators: Esri

Relocation Map

Following the Lives of those Evicted from their Homes in San Francisco

This project, as part of the Anti-Eviction Mapping Project's effort to document the "dispossession of San Francisco Bay Area residents upon gentrifying landsapces," is a collection of maps tracking the relocation of a group of San Francisco residents evicted from their homes in 2012. All data comes from the Eviction Defense Collaborative.

  • Date: May - June 2016
  • Collaborators: Anti-Eviction Mapping Project

Oakland Community Power

Crowd-sourced Mapping Tool for Building Community

This tool allows Oakland community members to create a colletive map of spaces that sustain the city. It was built with the Anti-Eviction Mapping Project to continue a process of engaging with local residents in the Bay Area to maintain community power.

  • Date: June 2015
  • Collaborators: Anti-Eviction Mapping Project

Living Wage Map

Visualizing the "Living Wage" Gap Across the US

Working with Esri, I partnered with MIT DUSP Professor Amy Glasmeier to visualize the “living wage” dataset, create static maps for Politico Magazine, and aid in the design of a custom application, now published by The Atlantic, Philadelphia Magazine, and Boston.com. Note: Custom App discontinued

  • Date: August 2015
  • Collaborators: Esri, MIT DUSP, Politico

Katrina +10

Mapping a Decade of Change in New Orleans.

"Katrina +10" is a series of maps I developed with Esri that follows the city of New Orleans through a decade of reshaping, rebuilding, and development post-Hurricane Katrina. The piece is published with Smithsonian Magazine.

  • Date: July 2015
  • Collaborators: Esri, Smithsonian

Income Inequality in American Cities

Exploring Income Divides

This project is a collection of seven narrative apps exploring income divides in major US cities, now published in Smithsonian Magazine.

  • Date: June - July 2015
  • Collaborators: Esri, Smithsonian

One Dollar Lots

A map of NYC land sold to developers for $1

Built for 596 Acres, this custom web tool maps out all land NYC sold—or plans to sell— to housing developers for $1 each. Pulling data from an aggregated database of city records and contracts, this mapping tool links users to critical information regarding each land sale and allows residents to imagine better uses for cheap public land.

  • Date: October 2017 - March 2018
  • Collaborators: 596 Acres

Housing New York: Local Affordability

How New York’s affordable apartments compare to neighborhood incomes

Published through Curbed New York, this data-driven investigation offers a glimpse into who can actually afford New York City’s “affordable” apartments. The article was featured in the New York Times's daily roundup column among other publications.

  • Date: January - March 2018
  • Collaborators: Curbed

AMI and the Affordability Gap

Explaining "Area Median Income" and how the City of New York uses it to quantify affordable housing

As part of a series for Curbed New York, this article combined interviews with HUD and NYC HPD along with in-house data analysis/visualization, all in an effort to explain what "afforable" means in the context of NYC housing development.

  • Date: October - December 2017
  • Collaborators: Curbed
